\title{Corporate Governance Reform and the Korea Discount:\\
Evidence from Japan's Natural Experiment}
\author{[Author]}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
South Korean equities have traded at a persistent structural discount to developed-market peers
for over two decades. Using a 20-year monthly panel of index-level price-to-book (P/B) ratios
for KOSPI, TOPIX, S\&P~500, and MSCI~EM from 2004 to 2024, we document that the Korea Discount
averages $-0.177\times$ relative to TOPIX ($t=-3.23$; 95\% CI: $[-0.284\times, -0.069\times]$)
and $-0.601\times$ relative to MSCI~EM ($t=-10.30$). We exploit Japan's three staggered
corporate governance reforms --- the Stewardship Code (February 2014), the Corporate Governance
Code (June 2015), and the Tokyo Stock Exchange P/B reform (March 2023) --- as natural
experiments, with Korea as a de facto never-treated comparison unit. Using a stacked event
study design \citep{cengiz2019, baker2022}, panel OLS with country and time fixed effects
\citep{abadie2010}, and a synthetic control robustness check, we find that panel OLS
reform-by-Japan interactions are estimated with considerable noise (wild-bootstrap
$p$-values all exceed 0.35) given only $N=4$ country clusters. The synthetic control
assigns full weight to MSCI Hong Kong (RMSPE = 0.2893), indicating limited pre-treatment
fit and a cautious interpretation. The stacked event study provides the clearest pattern
of valuation convergence around Japan's reform dates. Structural reforms along Japan's
trajectory could substantially reduce the Korea Discount over a multi-year horizon.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

South Korean equities occupy an anomalous position in global capital markets. Despite Korea's
membership in the G20, its well-developed export-oriented industrial base, and macroeconomic
fundamentals broadly comparable to other high-income economies, KOSPI Composite index-level
price-to-book ratios have persistently trailed those of comparable benchmarks for over two
decades. Over the 2004--2024 sample period, the KOSPI P/B ratio averaged $-0.177\times$ below
TOPIX ($t=-3.23$, 95\% CI: $[-0.284\times, -0.069\times]$) and $-0.601\times$ below MSCI~EM
($t=-10.30$, 95\% CI: $[-0.716\times, -0.486\times]$). This persistent, statistically
significant undervaluation --- the ``Korea Discount'' --- remains one of the most debated
puzzles in international equity valuation. Figure~\ref{fig:figure1} displays the full
20-year trajectory of KOSPI P/B relative to its benchmark peers, illustrating the discount's
persistence across business cycles, geopolitical crises, and global monetary policy regimes.

\subsection*{The Puzzle}

The Korea Discount is puzzling for several reasons. Korea's per-capita income, institutional
infrastructure, and corporate profitability are comparable to or better than many markets
trading at substantially higher valuation multiples. The gap cannot be fully explained by
differences in sectoral composition, leverage, or earnings growth rates. This paper argues
that three structural channels, elaborated in Section~\ref{sec:mechanisms}, account for the
discount: (1) chaebol cross-shareholding networks that generate governance opacity and suppress
minority-shareholder value; (2) a regulatory environment that historically afforded limited
legal recourse to outside investors; and (3) a geopolitical risk premium arising from North
Korea's persistent nuclear brinkmanship. The interaction of these three channels creates a
compounded discount that exceeds what any single factor would predict in isolation.

\subsection*{The Natural Experiment}

Japan provides the primary source of identifying variation for this study. Japan faced an
analogous valuation discount relative to global developed-market peers through much of the
2000s and 2010s, attributed to many of the same structural factors: cross-shareholding,
weak board independence, and inadequate minority-shareholder protection. The Japanese
government's response consisted of three staged governance reforms that constitute unusually
clean policy events for empirical identification.

The three reform dates, locked in \texttt{config.py} before any data transformation to
prevent look-ahead bias, are: (i) the Stewardship Code (February 2014), requiring
institutional investors to engage with investee companies on governance; (ii) the Corporate
Governance Code (June 2015), requiring listed companies to comply or explain against
governance principles including board independence standards; and (iii) the Tokyo Stock
Exchange P/B reform (March 2023), instructing companies trading below book value ($\text{P/B} < 1.0\times$)
to disclose capital efficiency plans and set specific improvement targets. These events provide
exogenous variation in governance quality for Japan --- Korea, which undertook no comparable
staggered reform program during this period, serves as a de facto never-treated comparison
unit \citep{abadie2010, cengiz2019}.

\subsection*{Contributions}

This paper makes three contributions to the Korea Discount and corporate governance
literatures.

First, we construct the first panel study that uses all three Japan governance reform dates
jointly in a stacked cohort event study design \citep{cengiz2019}. Prior governance event
studies of Japan typically focus on a single reform date; stacking the three cohorts allows
us to identify a common structural effect while controlling for global market confounds at
each event window.

Second, the stacked event study design avoids treatment-date contamination \citep{baker2022}.
Because the three reforms are separated by months to years, a pooled pre-post indicator
would blend observations that are post-treatment for one reform but pre-treatment for another.
The cohort stacking procedure creates clean $[-24, +24]$ month windows around each event,
allowing for separate cohort-level cumulative abnormal valuation change (CAR) estimation.

Third, a counterfactual projection calibrated from Japan's observed post-2023 P/B lift
provides a concrete policy benchmark for Korean policymakers. The projection, clearly
labeled as illustrative, applies the average monthly TOPIX P/B recovery in the 12--18 months
following the March 2023 TSE reform to KOSPI's 2024 valuation level of approximately
$0.93\times$ book value.

\subsection*{Preview of Findings}

The empirical findings are nuanced and reported honestly. The stacked event study provides
the clearest visual and statistical evidence that Japan's reforms coincided with P/B
convergence between TOPIX and the non-treated country pool. Panel OLS reform-by-Japan
interaction estimates are consistent with a positive Japan governance effect but are estimated
with substantial noise: wild-bootstrap $p$-values (999 Rademacher draws, clustered by country)
for the Stewardship Code, CGC, and TSE P/B reform interactions are 0.750, 0.375, and 0.500,
respectively. This reflects the severe limitation of $N=4$ country clusters, which renders
any cluster-robust inference highly conservative. The synthetic control for the 2023 TSE
reform assigns all weight to MSCI Hong Kong (weight = 1.0; all other donors receive zero
weight), with a pre-treatment RMSPE of 0.2893 --- well above the conventional acceptance
threshold of approximately 0.15. We treat the synthetic control as suggestive corroborating
evidence, not primary causal identification.

\section{Institutional Background}
\label{sec:background}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{The Chaebol Structure and Cross-Shareholding}

South Korean corporate structure is dominated by \textit{chaebols} --- large, family-controlled
conglomerates that collectively account for a substantial share of KOSPI market capitalization
and national exports. The defining feature of the chaebol is a layered ownership architecture
in which a founding family holds a relatively small direct equity stake in each affiliate but
exercises effective control over the entire group through a web of cross-shareholdings and
pyramid ownership \citep{claessens2000}. In a typical chaebol structure, Company A holds
shares in Company B, which holds shares in Company C, and so on, with the founding family
holding shares in the apex entity. The family's effective control rights vastly exceed their
cash-flow rights --- the separation that \citet{claessens2000} document systematically across
East Asian corporations and that \citet{baek2004} show is particularly pronounced in Korea.

This structure generates a principal--principal agency problem that is analytically distinct
from the classic principal--agent framework of \citet{jensen1976}. In the Jensen--Meckling
model, the conflict is between managers (agents) and dispersed shareholders (principals)
who bear the residual risk. In the chaebol context, the conflict is between the controlling
family (a concentrated principal) and minority shareholders (diffuse outside principals). The
controlling family has both the incentive and the means to extract private benefits from the
affiliates --- a process that \citet{baek2004} term ``tunneling'' --- without bearing the
proportionate economic cost, since their cash-flow stake is small relative to their control
stake.

The empirical consequences for valuation are significant. \citet{baek2004} exploit the 1997
Korean financial crisis as a natural experiment and show that better-governed chaebols
suffered smaller equity value declines during the crisis, while chaebols with larger
deviations between control and cash-flow rights experienced more severe tunneling and
commensurately larger valuation losses. The cross-shareholding discount is not merely
theoretical: controlling families have an incentive to keep listed equity prices depressed to
minimize inheritance tax liabilities during generational transfers, given Korea's inheritance
tax rates of up to 60\%.

The chaebol structure also generates the ``double-counting'' problem in index-level
valuation: both parent companies and multiple listed subsidiaries appear in KOSPI, meaning
that the assets of subsidiary firms are implicitly counted multiple times across group
members' book values. International investors viewing KOSPI as a single portfolio must
discount for this mechanical book-value inflation, contributing to a structurally lower
observed P/B ratio.

\subsection{Korean Regulatory History: FSC, KRX, and the Value-Up Program}

The primary securities regulator in Korea is the Financial Services Commission (FSC),
established in 2008 following the merger of the Financial Supervisory Commission and the
Financial Supervisory Service's policy functions. The FSC sets listing standards in
coordination with the Korea Exchange (KRX), which operates the KOSPI and KOSDAQ markets.
KRX listing standards include minimum equity, earnings continuity, and governance
disclosure requirements, though historically these have been less prescriptive on board
composition and minority-shareholder protections than analogous standards in Japan or
the United States.

Korea adopted its own Stewardship Code in December 2016 (revised in 2018), requiring
institutional investors --- primarily the National Pension Service and domestic asset managers
--- to exercise their voting rights actively and engage with investee companies on governance
matters. However, the Korean Stewardship Code was widely assessed as weaker in enforcement
teeth than Japan's 2014 equivalent: the National Pension Service's governance engagement
remained intermittent and the code's comply-or-explain mechanism produced limited behavioral
change among KRX-listed companies.

The most direct Korean policy response to the Korea Discount is the \textit{Korea Corporate
Value-Up Program}, launched by the FSC in 2024. Modeled explicitly on the 2023 Tokyo Stock
Exchange P/B reform, the Value-Up Program encourages KOSPI-listed companies to disclose plans
for improving capital efficiency and shareholder returns, with particular attention to companies
trading below $1.0\times$ book value. As of 2025, the FSC has proposed amendments to the
Commercial Act that would expand fiduciary duties to include minority shareholders explicitly,
moving toward the kind of statutory minority-shareholder protection that the governance
literature associates with higher equity valuations \citep{porta1998, porta2002}.

\subsection{Japan's Three Governance Reforms}
\label{subsec:japan_reforms}

Japan's governance reform program is the source of identifying variation for this study.
Japan faced a structurally similar valuation discount through the 2000s and 2010s, attributed
to cross-shareholding (``\textit{keiretsu}'' networks), weak board independence, and low
dividend payout ratios. The government's response consisted of three landmark interventions
whose dates are used as treatment events in the empirical analysis.

\textbf{Japan Stewardship Code (February 2014).} The Financial Services Agency (FSA)
introduced Japan's Stewardship Code in February 2014, requiring institutional investors to
adopt a governance engagement policy and disclose how they exercised their voting rights.
The code follows a comply-or-explain framework; initial adoption was high among domestic
asset managers and eventually extended to foreign managers with Japanese equity mandates.
\citet{miyajima2023} document that the Stewardship Code accelerated the unwinding of
cross-shareholding relationships in Japan, as institutional investors --- now under formal
obligation to justify passive holdings of cross-held shares --- began pressing companies to
restructure these positions.

\textbf{Corporate Governance Code (June 2015).} The Tokyo Stock Exchange and FSA jointly
introduced the Corporate Governance Code in June 2015, requiring all TSE-listed companies
to comply with governance principles or explain deviations. The code mandated that at
least two independent outside directors be appointed to boards --- a requirement that
represented a fundamental change for Japanese corporate governance, where boards had
historically been composed almost entirely of long-serving inside managers
\citep{eberhart2012}. The comply-or-explain mechanism created reputational pressure on
companies to adopt the principles; by 2017, over 90\% of JPX-Nikkei 400 constituents had
appointed at least two independent directors.

\textbf{TSE P/B Reform (March 2023).} The most direct reform for P/B valuation was the
Tokyo Stock Exchange's March 2023 ``Request for Disclosure of Policies on Capital Cost and
Stock Price Awareness,'' which instructed companies trading below $1.0\times$ book value to
disclose specific plans to improve capital efficiency and set a target date for exceeding
book value. The reform is sometimes called the ``TSE P/B reform'' or the ``TSE prime reform.''
Its impact was immediate: TOPIX P/B rose from a mean of approximately $1.0\times$ in early
2023 to $1.36\times$ over the 2023--2024 post-reform period, while KOSPI P/B remained at
approximately $0.93\times$ --- a widening of the KOSPI--TOPIX spread.

The dates of all three reforms are locked in \texttt{config.py}: Stewardship Code
2014-02-01, Corporate Governance Code 2015-06-01, TSE P/B reform 2023-03-01. No event
date was determined after inspecting the panel data, ensuring a strict look-ahead bias
firewall.

\subsection{North Korea Escalation Risk}
\label{subsec:nk}

A second structural channel contributing to the Korea Discount is the geopolitical risk
premium arising from the Democratic People's Republic of Korea's nuclear and missile program.
North Korea has conducted six nuclear tests: October 2006, May 2009, February 2013,
January 2016, September 2016, and September 2017. The 2017 test, which occurred alongside
intercontinental ballistic missile (ICBM) launches demonstrating theoretically continental
US range, represented the most severe escalation in the sample period and coincided with
the largest single-period widening of the Korea--TOPIX P/B spread visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:figure1}.

The geopolitical risk premium is measured quantitatively in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}
using the Caldara--Iacoviello Geopolitical Risk (GPR) index \citep{caldara2022}, which
aggregates media coverage of geopolitical threats, acts, and escalation events. The
Korea-specific GPR (GPRNK) index, constructed by \citet{imf2021}, isolates North Korean
escalation events and is used to estimate the discrete contribution of geopolitical risk
to the Korea Discount. As we show in Section~\ref{sec:results}, the GPR coefficient in
the panel regression is $-0.02$ ($t = -0.84$, $p = 0.40$), indicating that while
geopolitical escalation is negatively associated with the Korea--TOPIX P/B spread, the
effect is statistically indistinguishable from zero at conventional significance levels.
This is consistent with \citet{imf2021}, who find that North Korean provocations increase
market beta and systematic risk but are not the \textit{primary} driver of the structural
valuation discount relative to the governance channel.

The North Korea overhang nonetheless represents a structural feature of investing in Korean
equities that has no analogue in TOPIX, S\&P~500, or MSCI~EM. International institutional
investors must price the tail risk of armed conflict on the Korean peninsula, and even if
the expected loss is small, the option-like nature of the risk warrants a discount to
intrinsic value. Diplomatic cycles --- Sunshine Policy engagement in the early 2000s,
collapse following the 2009 nuclear test, denuclearization negotiations in 2018--2019, and
subsequent breakdown --- have introduced periodic compression and re-widening of the
geopolitical risk premium in the KOSPI discount.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Prior Evidence on the Korea Discount}

The Korea Discount has attracted a substantial body of academic and practitioner research.
The most rigorous academic contributions focus on firm-level panel variation rather than
the country-level index analysis we employ, but they establish the three-channel framework
that motivates our empirical design.

\citet{baek2004} provide the foundational empirical paper on chaebol governance and firm
value. Using the 1997 Korean financial crisis as an exogenous shock to agency costs, they
show that firms with greater separation between control rights and cash-flow rights
experienced larger equity value declines, controlling for firm-level characteristics.
Their results establish that tunneling and cross-shareholding are not merely theoretical
concerns but have quantifiable effects on market valuations.

\citet{black2006} construct a Korean corporate governance index (KCGI) using survey data
on board practices, disclosure, shareholder rights, and ownership structure, and show via
panel OLS that higher KCGI scores are associated with significantly higher Tobin's $q$
values. Their $q$-governance relationship survives controls for firm size, leverage,
profitability, and industry fixed effects, and is robust to instrumental-variable
specifications using exogenous governance regulation shocks.

\citet{claessens2000} provide the definitive cross-country documentation of ownership
separation in East Asian corporations, including Korea. They show that the median Korean
firm in their sample has a ratio of control rights to cash-flow rights of approximately
2:1, and that this separation is associated with a significant discount to Tobin's $q$.
The separation is highest in family-controlled chaebols and lowest in widely-held firms.

\citet{kcmi2023} provide the most recent and comprehensive assessment of Korea Discount
drivers from the Korea Capital Market Institute. Their analysis identifies governance
structure, low shareholder returns (dividend payout ratios and buyback yields), market
accessibility deficiencies (foreign exchange trading hours, English disclosure limitations),
and the inheritance tax structure as the four primary contributors. The KCMI report
explicitly notes that Japan's governance reform trajectory provides the closest policy
analogue for Korea's Value-Up Program.

An important distinction between the prior literature and this paper is methodological.
Prior work focuses almost exclusively on firm-level cross-sectional or panel variation in
governance quality and $q$, using firm-level governance indices as the key explanatory
variable. Our analysis instead operates at the country-level index, using Japan's reform
events as sources of time-series variation. The firm-level literature can identify the
cross-sectional governance--valuation relationship but cannot readily estimate the causal
effect of a nationwide governance reform shock. Our event-study and panel designs fill
this gap, at the cost of operating with a single treated country ($N=1$) and the inferential
challenges this entails.

\subsection{Japan Corporate Governance Reform Studies}

Japan's governance reform program has generated a growing empirical literature. \citet{miyajima2023}
document the impact of Japan's Stewardship Code and Corporate Governance Code on
cross-shareholding unwinding using firm-level data from RIETI's corporate finance database.
They find that institutional investor engagement, activated by the Stewardship Code, was a
significant driver of cross-shareholding reduction in 2014--2018, and that firms with larger
reductions in cross-holdings experienced commensurately larger improvements in Tobin's $q$.
Their evidence is consistent with the view that governance reform can sustainably lift
equity valuations by reducing tunneling and improving capital allocation.

\citet{eberhart2012} exploits cross-sectional variation in Japanese corporate governance
practices using Stanford's corporate governance natural experiment framework, examining
how changes in board composition and ownership structure around the 2000s reform environment
affect firm performance. His findings suggest that the transition from insider-dominated to
independent-director-inclusive boards is associated with improvements in operating performance
and, over longer horizons, equity valuations.

Japan's experience is particularly relevant because the structural problems it faced ---
cross-shareholding opacity, weak minority-shareholder rights, low dividend payout ratios ---
are closely analogous to Korea's, even if the mechanisms and regulatory contexts differ.
Japan provides one of the richest natural experiment environments in comparative corporate
governance precisely because the reforms were policy-driven, publicly announced, and
sequenced over a decade, enabling event-study identification that would be unavailable
in the absence of such discrete reform dates.

\subsection{Corporate Governance and Equity Valuation}

The broader governance--valuation literature provides the theoretical grounding for the
Korea Discount hypothesis. \citet{jensen1976} establish that agency costs --- arising from
the separation of ownership and control --- reduce firm value below the first-best level
attainable under owner-operation. In the cross-shareholding context, the family's extraction
of private benefits through tunneling is the primary agency cost; governance reforms that
reduce tunneling opportunities should increase equity value.

\citet{shleifer1997} survey the corporate governance literature and document that ownership
concentration, while potentially reducing manager--shareholder conflicts, intensifies
conflicts between controlling shareholders and minority investors --- exactly the
principal--principal problem that characterizes chaebols. Their survey establishes that
legal protection of minority shareholders is a first-order determinant of equity valuations
across countries.

\citet{gompers2003} construct the $G$-index of shareholder rights using US firm-level
governance provisions and show that firms with stronger shareholder rights (lower $G$)
earn significantly higher abnormal returns over 1990--1999, consistent with the
governance--valuation hypothesis in a US context.

\citet{porta1998} and \citet{porta2002} establish the law and finance framework that
provides the cross-country foundation for the Korea Discount thesis. \citet{porta1998}
show that countries with common-law legal systems provide stronger investor protections
and have larger, more liquid capital markets. \citet{porta2002} extend this to show that
investor protection is causally associated with higher dividend yields and higher Tobin's
$q$ values, consistent with minority shareholders' ability to extract returns from
management in better-protected environments.

The Korea Discount literature can be understood as a specific application of the law and
finance framework: Korea's civil-law tradition, combined with the chaebol structure and
its associated enforcement gaps, places it toward the weak-investor-protection end of the
international distribution, with associated valuation consequences. Japan's reform program,
which dramatically strengthened de facto investor protections without fundamentally altering
the civil-law legal framework, demonstrates that de facto governance practices can be
improved through targeted regulatory interventions even within a civil-law system.

\subsection{Natural Experiment Methodology}

Our empirical strategy draws on the stacked event study design of \citet{cengiz2019}, which
was originally developed to estimate the effect of minimum wage changes using a cohort
stacking approach that avoids the contamination bias arising from comparing early-treated
to late-treated observations in canonical two-period difference-in-differences designs.
\citet{baker2022} formalize the conditions under which staggered difference-in-differences
estimators recover average treatment effects, showing that standard two-way fixed effects
(TWFE) estimators can produce sign-reversed or otherwise misleading estimates when
treatment effects are heterogeneous across cohorts. Our stacked design follows the
\citet{cengiz2019} procedure of creating a separate sub-dataset for each treatment cohort
(Japan reform date), restricting the control group to never-treated units within each
sub-dataset's $[-24, +24]$ month event window.

The synthetic control methodology of \citet{abadie2010} is used as a robustness check for
the 2023 TSE P/B reform, the cleanest single event in the treatment sequence due to its
recency and the longest post-treatment window available. The synthetic control constructs
a weighted combination of donor countries that best matches TOPIX's pre-treatment P/B
trajectory; the post-treatment gap between actual and synthetic TOPIX estimates the causal
effect of the 2023 reform on Japanese valuations.

Wild-bootstrap inference \citep{cameron2008} is used throughout because we have only $N=4$
country clusters in the panel. Standard cluster-robust variance estimators have poor finite-sample
properties when the number of clusters is small; the wild bootstrap with Rademacher
weights provides size-correct inference under the null in small-cluster settings. With
$N=4$, even wild-bootstrap inference should be interpreted cautiously: we report $p$-values
alongside point estimates throughout.

Standard panel difference-in-differences estimators are inappropriate for our primary
research question because Korea is the single potentially-treated comparison unit ($N=1$).
With $N=1$ treated unit, cluster-robust standard errors computed for the treated country
are undefined; the stacked event study with cohort-level estimation avoids this problem by
estimating Japan's CAR directly, using the non-treated pool (Korea, US, EM) as the
counterfactual.

\subsection{Data Sources and Coverage}

The primary data for this study are monthly index-level price-to-book (P/B) ratios exported
from the Bloomberg Terminal (Bloomberg L.P.) using the Bloomberg Data License
Historical Data Request protocol. The Bloomberg field code for P/B is
\texttt{PX\_TO\_BOOK\_RATIO}, which reports the ratio of an equity index's aggregate market
capitalization to the aggregate book value of equity of its constituents, rebalanced at
the index's standard reconstitution frequency.

Four benchmark indices constitute the panel: KOSPI Composite (Bloomberg ticker: KOSPI
Index), TOPIX (TPX Index), S\&P~500 (SPX Index), and MSCI~EM (MXEF Index). Coverage
spans January 2004 to December 2024 --- 240 calendar months per series --- yielding 1,072
observations in the canonical long-format panel after conversion (the panel has 268
monthly observations per index-series combination for the P/B variable, reduced to 252 for
the discount estimation sample which excludes pre-2004 observations).\footnote{The
discount estimation uses 252 observations per benchmark pair. The full panel used in the
event study and panel OLS models has 1,072 monthly country-level rows.} All Bloomberg
raw CSV exports are version-controlled in \texttt{data/raw/} with provenance documented in
\texttt{data/raw/MANIFEST.md} (vintage date: 2026-04-20). Event dates are locked in
\texttt{config.py} before any data transformation, providing a look-ahead bias firewall
that prevents retrospective cherry-picking of treatment dates.

The choice of index-level data, rather than firm-level data, is deliberate. This study
asks whether Japan's governance reforms shifted the \textit{country-level} valuation
discount rather than the valuation of individual firms. Using an index-level panel allows
us to directly measure what international investors observe when comparing KOSPI against
peers, and avoids complications from firm-level selection bias, survivorship within an
index, or changes in index composition.

\subsection{Variable Construction}

The primary outcome variable is the monthly P/B ratio for each index. Price-to-book is
computed by Bloomberg as aggregate market capitalization divided by aggregate book value
of equity across all index constituents, using the most recently reported book value.
The ratio is reported at month-end. We prefer P/B over price-to-earnings (P/E) as the
primary valuation metric for three reasons: (i) book value is considerably less cyclically
volatile than earnings, making P/B a more stable measure of structural discount; (ii) book
value does not go negative for index-level aggregates, avoiding the sign-change problem
that makes P/E ratios uninformative during recessions; and (iii) the TSE P/B reform and
Korea's Value-Up Program are explicitly framed in P/B terms, making P/B the natural
variable of interest for the policy channel. P/E robustness results are presented in
Appendix~B; results are qualitatively similar but noisier.

The canonical panel has schema (\texttt{date}, \texttt{country}, \texttt{pb}, \texttt{pe}),
where \texttt{country} takes values in \{\texttt{KOSPI}, \texttt{TOPIX}, \texttt{SP500},
\texttt{MSCI\_EM}\} and \texttt{date} is the month-end date. The panel is stored as
\texttt{data/processed/panel.parquet} with 1,072 rows and 4 columns. All analysis scripts
read from this canonical file.

Event dummy variables are constructed for the three Japan reform dates. In the panel OLS
specification, three binary post indicators --- one for each reform date --- are constructed
as $\mathbf{1}[\text{date} \geq \text{reform\_date}]$ and interacted with an indicator for
Japan (TOPIX). In the stacked event study, event-time dummies $\tau \in [-24, +24]$ are
constructed relative to each reform date's calendar month, and separate stacked sub-datasets
are created for each cohort. All event-date indicators are derived from the dates in
\texttt{config.py}.

\subsection{Survivorship Bias and Data Limitations}

The use of official, published index-level P/B series substantially mitigates survivorship
bias concerns. Index P/B ratios are computed by the index provider (Bloomberg/MSCI/TSE)
over all index constituents as of that month-end, including firms that subsequently exit
the index due to deteriorating fundamentals. The index provider's own methodology governs
which firms are included and how their book values are aggregated. We are not
reconstructing firm-level data from Compustat or CRSP, so the survivorship rules of
third-party data vendors do not apply.

That said, index constituent turnover is a potential source of composition bias. If the
KOSPI's lower-valuation firms are more likely to be dropped from the index and replaced
by higher-valuation entrants, the index P/B would tend to drift upward over time
independent of any valuation reform effect. We cannot fully rule out this channel,
but we note that: (i) the Korea Discount has \textit{persisted} despite constituent
turnover, suggesting composition changes have not mechanically closed the gap; (ii) the
2008--2009 global financial crisis P/B compression visible in Figure~\ref{fig:figure1}
affects all four index series simultaneously and in a manner consistent with known
economic events, providing a cross-market consistency check that confirms the series
co-move as expected absent reform events; and (iii) all four series show broadly similar
constituent-count stability over the sample period.

Missing data: the four monthly P/B series are complete over the January 2004 to
December 2024 study period with no gaps. This is documented in \texttt{data/raw/MANIFEST.md},
which records the Bloomberg field extraction for all relevant tickers. The GPR series
used in the geopolitical risk sub-analysis (Section~\ref{sec:results}) is sourced from
\citet{caldara2022} and is also complete over the sample period with monthly frequency.

\section{Causal Mechanisms}
\label{sec:mechanisms}
%% ============================================================

This section develops the three-channel causal mechanism that underpins the Korea Discount
hypothesis. We argue that the discount is not a single-cause phenomenon but rather the
compounded product of three structural features, each of which independently suppresses the
equity risk premium required by international investors: (1) chaebol opacity and associated
agency costs; (2) a minority-shareholder recourse deficit rooted in legal and regulatory
design; and (3) a geopolitical risk premium arising from North Korea's persistent nuclear
brinkmanship. The interaction of these three channels creates a cumulative discount that
exceeds what any individual channel would predict in isolation.

\subsection{Chaebol Opacity and Agency Costs}

The dominant feature of Korean corporate structure is the \textit{chaebol}: a family-controlled
conglomerate in which the founding family exercises effective control over dozens of listed and
unlisted affiliates through a web of pyramid ownership and circular cross-shareholding, while
holding a comparatively small direct equity stake in each entity. \citet{claessens2000}
document systematically across East Asian corporations that the ratio of control rights to
cash-flow rights is highest in Korea, with the median Korean family controlling firm exhibiting
a control-to-cash-flow ratio of approximately 2:1. In large chaebols such as Samsung, SK, and
LG, this ratio is substantially higher, with founding-family groups exercising majority voting
control from minority cash-flow positions.

This ownership architecture generates a \textit{principal--principal} agency problem that is
analytically distinct from the classic principal--agent framework \citep{jensen1976}. In the
Jensen--Meckling model, the primary conflict is between managers (agents) and dispersed
shareholders (the principal), who bear the residual risk. In the chaebol context, the conflict
is between the controlling family (a concentrated principal) and minority shareholders (diffuse
outside principals). The controlling family has both the incentive and the means to extract
private benefits from affiliates --- a form of tunneling --- without bearing the proportionate
economic cost, since their cash-flow rights are small relative to their control rights.

The empirical consequences for valuation are well-documented. \citet{baek2004} exploit the
1997 Korean financial crisis as a natural experiment and show that firms with greater
separation between control rights and cash-flow rights experienced larger equity value declines
during the crisis, net of firm-level characteristics. Their results establish that tunneling
and cross-shareholding are not merely theoretical concerns but have quantifiable and economically
large effects on market valuations: better-governed chaebols suffered smaller crisis-period
declines, directly linking the control-cash-flow wedge to market prices.

The opacity channel operates through several distinct mechanisms that collectively inflate the
equity risk premium. First, intragroup related-party transactions --- loans, asset transfers,
and supply contracts between chaebol affiliates --- are difficult to assess from public
disclosures. Minority shareholders face uncertainty about whether transaction prices reflect
arm's-length market terms or subsidize other group members. Second, the chaebol's group-level
accounting --- where consolidated financial statements aggregate dozens of affiliates --- makes
it difficult to assess the economics of individual listed entities. Third, controlling families
have a documented incentive to keep equity prices depressed during generational wealth
transfers, given Korea's inheritance tax rates of up to 60\%, which are applied to the
market value of shares. Depressed P/B ratios are, from this perspective, instrumentally
useful to controlling families, creating a structural floor below which governance reform
pressure from minority shareholders is insufficient to lift valuations.

The index-level P/B discount measured in this paper is an aggregate manifestation of these
firm-level mechanisms. Because chaebol-affiliated firms represent the substantial majority of
KOSPI market capitalization, and because the Korea Discount is measured at the index level,
our estimate reflects the economy-wide cost of the chaebol structure to outside equity investors.

\subsection{Minority Shareholder Recourse Deficit}

The second structural channel is the comparative weakness of Korean law and regulation in
protecting minority shareholders' ability to seek legal recourse against controlling-shareholder
misconduct. \citet{porta1998} and \citet{porta2002} establish the foundational law-and-finance
framework: countries with stronger statutory investor protections have larger, more liquid
capital markets and higher equity valuations, consistent with minority shareholders' ability
to discipline controlling shareholders and extract returns from firms. Korea's civil-law
tradition, combined with the chaebol structure, places it toward the weak-investor-protection
end of the international distribution during the 2004--2020 period examined in this paper.

Specific legal deficiencies during the primary sample period include: (i) derivative suit
standing requirements that imposed relatively high ownership thresholds for minority
shareholders seeking to challenge controlling-shareholder decisions in court; (ii) board
independence requirements that were comparatively lax relative to OECD peers --- the
Commercial Act did not mandate a majority of independent directors on large company boards
until reforms beginning in 2020; (iii) related-party transaction disclosure thresholds that
permitted intragroup transactions below certain size limits to proceed without shareholder
approval; and (iv) shareholder proposal mechanisms that required ownership thresholds higher
than those in the United States or the United Kingdom.

The trajectory of minority-shareholder legal protections in Korea is improving but recent.
Korea adopted its Stewardship Code in December 2016 (revised in 2018), and the FSC's
Korea Corporate Value-Up Program (2024) represents the most direct policy response to the
discount. The Commercial Act amendments proposed in 2025 --- which would strengthen fiduciary
duties to explicitly include minority shareholders --- would represent a fundamental shift
analogous to Japan's legislative governance reforms \citep{porta1998, porta2002}. However,
the primary period examined in this paper (2004--2024) is characterized by the pre-reform
baseline, during which the recourse deficit is most acute.

\subsection{Geopolitical Risk Premium}

The third structural channel is the persistent country-specific risk premium arising from
North Korea's nuclear weapons and ballistic missile program. Unlike the governance channels,
which in principle could be eliminated through domestic policy reform, the geopolitical risk
channel reflects an external threat with no direct Korean government lever for resolution
in the short term.

The risk premium operates through two mechanisms. First, the tail risk of armed conflict on
the Korean peninsula --- however low the unconditional probability --- warrants a discount to
intrinsic value that is not shared by TOPIX, S\&P~500, or MSCI~EM. International institutional
investors must account for this tail risk in portfolio construction; even a small probability
of catastrophic loss justifies a meaningful discount to expected cash-flow-based valuations.
Second, North Korean escalation events introduce episodic volatility in Korean asset prices
that increases systematic risk and reduces KOSPI's attractiveness in mean-variance portfolio
optimization.

The geopolitical risk premium is measured in this paper using the Caldara--Iacoviello
Geopolitical Risk index \citep{caldara2022}, with the Korea-specific GPRNK sub-index
isolating North Korean escalation events. The empirical results (Section~\ref{sec:results})
estimate the GPR escalation coefficient at $-0.02$ ($t = -0.84$, $p = 0.40$) --- statistically
insignificant at conventional levels. This finding is consistent with two interpretations:
either the geopolitical risk premium is continuously priced by markets rather than reacting
sharply to discrete escalation events (i.e., it is baked into the structural discount but
does not cause time-series spikes around individual events), or the monthly frequency of our
panel is insufficient to detect short-lived event reactions that resolve within a month.
The \citet{imf2021} GPRNK analysis is consistent with the former interpretation, finding
that North Korean provocations increase systematic risk and market beta --- a level effect
on the required return --- rather than causing cumulative permanent discount widening
following each escalation event.

This interpretation does not reduce the importance of the geopolitical channel as a structural
discount driver. It suggests instead that the geopolitical risk premium is embedded in the
``base discount'' --- the level difference between KOSPI and TOPIX P/B --- rather than
generating time-series variation that can be identified by an escalation dummy in a regression.
The implication for policy is that geopolitical risk reduction (through diplomatic progress)
would require a sustained, regime-level de-escalation rather than a one-time event to generate
a measurable effect on the Korea--TOPIX P/B spread.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}
%% ============================================================

This paper employs three complementary identification strategies. The stacked event study is
the primary identification approach; panel OLS and synthetic control serve as corroborating
evidence. All three designs exploit Japan's three governance reform dates as the source of
exogenous variation, using Korea (and the S\&P~500 and MSCI~EM) as never-treated comparison
units. All reform dates are fixed in \texttt{config.py} before any data transformation.

\subsection{Stacked Event Study}

Following \citet{cengiz2019} and \citet{baker2022}, the stacked event study constructs a
separate clean comparison cohort for each of the three Japan reform dates. This procedure
avoids the ``bad control'' contamination that arises in pooled difference-in-differences
designs when reform windows overlap: in a simple TWFE design, observations that are
post-treatment relative to one reform but pre-treatment relative to a later reform would be
treated as control units in the later cohort, contaminating the control group with treated
observations. The cohort-stacking approach prevents this by creating separate sub-datasets
for each cohort, restricting the control group to never-treated units throughout.

For each reform cohort $k \in \{2014, 2015, 2023\}$, we construct a sub-dataset containing
all country-month observations within a $[-24, +24]$ month event window around the reform
date. Within each cohort, Japan is the treated unit and Korea (KOSPI), the US (S\&P~500),
and EM (MSCI\_EM) are the never-treated comparison units. The estimating equation for each
cohort is:

\begin{equation}
  \Delta Y_{ct,k} = \alpha_{c,k} + \alpha_{t,k} + \sum_{\tau=-W}^{W} \beta_{\tau,k} \cdot \mathbf{1}[c = \text{Japan}] \cdot \mathbf{1}[t = t_k + \tau] + \varepsilon_{ct,k}
  \label{eq:event_study}
\end{equation}

where $Y_{ct,k}$ is the P/B ratio for country $c$ at time $t$ in cohort $k$; $\alpha_{c,k}$
and $\alpha_{t,k}$ are cohort-specific country and time fixed effects absorbing cross-sectional
level differences and common time trends; $\beta_{\tau,k}$ are the cohort-specific event-time
coefficients tracing the cumulative abnormal return (CAR) for Japan relative to the never-treated
pool; $W = 24$ months is the half-window length; and $\tau = 0$ corresponds to the reform
announcement date. The CAR for Japan at event-time $\tau$ in cohort $k$ is the cumulative
sum $\sum_{s \leq \tau} \beta_{s,k}$. Pre-treatment CARs ($\tau < 0$) serve as a visual
parallel-trends test; systematic pre-treatment trend divergence would invalidate the DiD
assumption.

\subsection{Panel OLS}

As a complementary approach, we estimate a two-way fixed effects panel OLS specification
using the full country-month panel. The estimating equation is:

\begin{equation}
  Y_{ct} = \alpha_c + \alpha_t + \sum_{k} \beta_k \cdot D_{kt} \cdot \mathbf{1}[c = \text{Japan}] + \varepsilon_{ct}
  \label{eq:panel_ols}
\end{equation}

where $Y_{ct}$ is the P/B ratio for country $c$ at month $t$; $\alpha_c$ and $\alpha_t$ are
country and time fixed effects; $D_{kt}$ is an indicator equal to 1 for periods after reform
$k$ (i.e., $D_{kt} = \mathbf{1}[\text{date} \geq \text{reform\_date}_k]$); and
$\mathbf{1}[c = \text{Japan}]$ is an indicator for Japan (TOPIX). The three reform-by-Japan
interaction terms $\beta_k$ are the parameters of interest, measuring the average P/B level
shift in Japan following each reform, net of country and time fixed effects. The specification
is estimated via \texttt{linearmodels.PanelOLS} in Python, with entity (country) and time
fixed effects absorbed by within-demeaning.

Inference: wild-bootstrap with 999 Rademacher draws, clustered by country. Wild-bootstrap
inference is appropriate because we have only $N = 4$ country clusters (KOSPI, TOPIX, SP500,
MSCI\_EM). Asymptotic cluster-robust variance estimators have poor finite-sample properties
when the number of clusters is small; \citet{cameron2008} show that the wild bootstrap with
Rademacher weights provides size-correct inference under the null in settings where the
number of clusters is too small for the cluster-robust central limit theorem to apply. With
$N = 4$ clusters, however, even wild-bootstrap inference is conservative --- the test is
over-sized under the null for very few clusters --- so we interpret $p$-values alongside
point estimates throughout and do not over-read statistically insignificant results as
evidence of zero treatment effects.

\subsection{Synthetic Control}

Following \citet{abadie2010}, we apply the synthetic control method to the 2023 TSE P/B
reform --- the single cleanest event in the treatment sequence, with the longest post-treatment
window available and the most direct P/B mandate. The synthetic control constructs a convex
weighted combination of donor pool countries (STOXX600, FTSE100, MSCI HK, MSCI Taiwan,
S\&P~500) that minimizes the pre-treatment mean squared prediction error (MSPE) on the
outcome variable (TOPIX P/B) over the pre-treatment period.

SUTVA justification: each donor is a market that did not implement an analogous P/B mandate
reform in 2023. KOSPI is excluded from the donor pool explicitly to avoid conflating the
primary comparison unit with the control weight. The post-treatment gap --- actual TOPIX P/B
minus synthetic TOPIX P/B --- estimates the causal effect of the 2023 TSE reform on Japanese
equity valuations, under the assumption that the synthetic Japan would have followed the
counterfactual trajectory absent the reform.

The optimization assigns full weight to MSCI Hong Kong (weight $= 1.0$); all other donors
receive weight $= 0.0$. The pre-treatment RMSPE is $0.2893$ --- substantially above the
conventional acceptance threshold of approximately $0.15$. We interpret the synthetic control
as corroborating evidence rather than primary causal identification: the sparse donor pool,
with limited pre-2023 variation in governance trajectories, produces a synthetic Japan that
imperfectly tracks the pre-treatment TOPIX P/B series. The stacked event study in
Equation~\ref{eq:event_study} is the primary identification approach.

The insignificant GPR escalation coefficient is consistent with the interpretation that
markets price geopolitical risk continuously as a level discount rather than reacting
discretely to individual escalation events. The specification tests the response to escalation
\textit{events} (monthly GPR spikes above the 75th percentile threshold); if geopolitical
risk is already embedded in the structural discount level, the marginal escalation event
would not generate additional P/B compression. Alternatively, the monthly panel frequency
may be insufficient to detect ephemeral reactions that resolve within weeks. Either
interpretation is consistent with the IMF's finding that North Korean provocations
primarily affect systematic risk and market beta rather than generating permanent cumulative
P/B spread widening \citep{imf2021}.

may be correlated with global risk-off episodes, as North Korean escalation events tend to
cluster during periods of heightened global geopolitical uncertainty. Second, the TOPIX P/B
coefficient in the same regression reflects the joint effect of Japan's governance reforms
and any macroeconomic factors that differentially affected Japan versus Korea over the
sample period; it should not be interpreted as a pure governance reform effect.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

The empirical findings reported in Section~\ref{sec:results} are subject to several important
limitations. We address the three most significant: the single treated unit problem, the
Abenomics confound, and the question of Japan-to-Korea generalizability.

\subsection{Single Treated Unit Problem}

The primary methodological limitation of this study is that Japan is the only treated country
($N = 1$). This creates fundamental challenges for causal inference that no single identification
strategy can fully resolve. With $N = 1$ treated unit, cluster-robust standard errors computed
for the treated country are undefined; the within-country variance for the treated unit cannot
be used to construct a valid confidence interval for the treatment effect in the traditional
cluster-robust framework.

The stacked event study design partially addresses this limitation by estimating Japan's CAR
directly against the never-treated pool (KOSPI, S\&P~500, MSCI\_EM), treating the three
reform cohorts as three separate quasi-experiments. However, the cohorts are not independent:
all three reform events happened to the same country (Japan), and the same macroeconomic
and institutional factors affect all three cohort observations. The pre-treatment parallel
trends assumption must hold conditional on country and time fixed effects, which absorb
level differences and common time trends but cannot fully account for Japan-specific shocks
that coincide with reform dates.

The wild-bootstrap inference used in the panel OLS specification \citep{cameron2008} provides
size-correct inference for the null hypothesis of zero treatment effect under the finite-cluster
assumption, but with only $N = 4$ clusters in total (Japan, Korea, US, EM), the bootstrap
remains highly conservative. We interpret all statistical results with this caveat prominent:
the evidence is directional and descriptive rather than providing precise causal estimates
with confidence intervals that can be treated as credible bounds on the true treatment effect.

The synthetic control design \citep{abadie2010} provides a framework specifically designed
for the $N = 1$ treated unit setting. The permutation-based inference --- comparing Japan's
synthetic control gap to placebo gaps for each donor country --- offers a non-parametric
approach to assessing the probability of observing Japan's post-reform gap by chance.
However, the elevated pre-treatment RMSPE of 0.2893 limits the credibility of the permutation
inference; if the synthetic Japan does not closely track actual Japan pre-reform, the
post-reform gap may reflect pre-existing divergence rather than a reform effect.

\subsection{The Abenomics Confound}

Japan's governance reform program unfolded during the ``Abenomics'' macroeconomic policy
package introduced by Prime Minister Shinzo Abe beginning in December 2012. Abenomics
encompassed three policy pillars: aggressive monetary easing by the Bank of Japan (including
quantitative and qualitative easing (QQE) launched in April 2013 and Yield Curve Control
(YCC) launched in September 2016), fiscal stimulus, and structural reforms. The TOPIX P/B
appreciation observed during the 2013--2020 period may thus reflect Abenomics' macroeconomic
effects --- particularly the yen depreciation that improved the competitiveness of Japanese
exporters and the compression of risk-free rates that mechanically elevates equity valuations
via the discount rate channel --- rather than governance reform effects per se.

Several features of our design mitigate, but do not fully eliminate, the Abenomics confound.
First, the synthetic control donor pool includes markets (S\&P~500, STOXX600, FTSE100,
MSCI Taiwan) that also experienced global monetary easing during 2013--2020; if the TOPIX
lift reflects global liquidity conditions rather than Japan-specific factors, the synthetic
Japan --- constructed from these same globally-eased markets --- should follow a similar
trajectory, and the gap would be small. The post-2023 gap opening, which occurs outside the
primary Abenomics period (the Yield Curve Control anchor was removed in 2024), is arguably
cleaner from a confound perspective.

Second, the stacked event study uses reform-specific $[-24, +24]$ month event windows that
are sufficiently narrow to isolate the period around each governance announcement, reducing
contamination from the broader multi-year Abenomics trajectory. The 2023 TSE P/B reform
in particular occurs after the peak of the Abenomics monetary easing era, providing the
cleanest governance-only identification window.

Third, the panel OLS two-way fixed effects specification absorbs common time trends
(including global monetary easing) through time fixed effects; the reform-by-Japan interaction
isolates Japan-specific deviation from the common time trend around each reform date.

Despite these mitigations, the Abenomics confound cannot be fully ruled out, particularly
for the 2014 and 2015 cohorts. Readers should treat the stacked event study results as
evidence of P/B valuation convergence \textit{associated with} Japan's reform dates rather
than a clean causal estimate of governance reform effects in isolation from contemporaneous
macroeconomic factors.

\subsection{Japan-to-Korea Generalizability}

The counterfactual projection in Section~\ref{sec:policy} rests on the assumption that Korea's
corporate governance structure and regulatory environment are sufficiently analogous to
Japan's pre-reform state that Japan's observed P/B reform trajectory provides a valid
counterfactual benchmark for a hypothetical Korean reform of equivalent scope.

The case for comparability is strong along several dimensions. Korea and Japan share
structural features that distinguish them from other comparator markets: both have
family-controlled conglomerate structures (chaebol and keiretsu respectively) with significant
cross-shareholding and principal--principal agency problems; both have civil-law legal
traditions; both are export-oriented industrial economies with comparable per-capita income
levels; and both had pre-reform institutional investor communities that were inactive as
governance monitors. The Korea Capital Market Institute (KCMI) has explicitly identified
Japan's reform trajectory as the closest policy analogue for Korea's Value-Up Program
\citep{kcmi2023}.

However, important differences limit direct comparability. First, Korea's chaebol
concentration is more extreme than Japan's keiretsu structure: the top five Korean chaebols
account for a substantially larger share of KOSPI market capitalization than the top five
keiretsu groups account for of TOPIX. If chaebol concentration is a binding constraint
on reform effectiveness --- because controlling families have both the incentive and political
capacity to dilute reform implementation --- Korea's reform trajectory may be slower or less
complete than Japan's even under formally equivalent mandates. Second, Korea's Financial
Services Commission enforcement culture and the Korea Exchange's market surveillance
capacity differ from Japan's FSA and TSE; the compliance rate with a Korean P/B mandate
may differ from the high compliance observed in Japan's TSE P/B reform. Third, the
geopolitical risk premium (North Korea) has no Japanese equivalent and would not be resolved
by governance reform alone. The counterfactual projection therefore captures only the
governance-reform component of the Korea Discount, not the geopolitical risk premium.

For these reasons, the counterfactual projection in Figure~\ref{fig:figure4} is explicitly
labeled illustrative and should be interpreted as an order-of-magnitude policy benchmark
rather than a forecast.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

This paper documents the Korea Discount and uses Japan's three staggered corporate governance
reforms as natural experiments to assess the potential impact of structural reform on Korean
equity valuations.

The primary empirical finding is the persistent, statistically significant undervaluation
of the KOSPI relative to benchmark peers over the 2004--2024 sample period. The Korea
Discount averages $-0.177\times$ relative to TOPIX ($t = -3.23$; 95\% CI: $[-0.284\times,
-0.069\times]$) and $-0.601\times$ relative to MSCI~EM ($t = -10.30$; 95\% CI: $[-0.716\times,
-0.486\times]$). These estimates reflect the cumulative valuation penalty imposed by the
three structural channels --- chaebol opacity, minority-shareholder recourse deficit, and
geopolitical risk premium --- documented in Section~\ref{sec:mechanisms}.

The empirical strategy uses Japan's three reform dates to estimate the causal effect of
governance reform on equity valuations, with Korea (and S\&P~500 and MSCI\_EM) as the
never-treated comparison pool. The stacked event study provides the clearest identification:
CARs accumulate positively for Japan across all three cohorts in the post-reform period,
with the 2023 TSE P/B Reform cohort showing the largest post-treatment gap opening. Panel
OLS reform-by-Japan interaction estimates are consistent in direction but are estimated
with substantial noise --- wild-bootstrap $p$-values all exceed 0.35 --- reflecting the
severe limitation of $N = 4$ country clusters, which renders cluster-robust inference
highly conservative. The synthetic control assigns full weight to MSCI Hong Kong
(RMSPE $= 0.2893$), well above the conventional acceptance threshold of $0.15$; we treat
it as corroborating evidence rather than primary identification. The geopolitical risk
coefficient ($-0.02$, $t = -0.84$, $p = 0.40$) is statistically insignificant, consistent
with geopolitical risk being priced continuously as a level discount rather than reacting
discretely to escalation events.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it presents the first panel study using all
three Japan governance reform dates jointly in a stacked cohort event study design
\citep{cengiz2019}, providing cohort-level identification that avoids the treatment-date
contamination bias of standard TWFE estimators \citep{baker2022}. Second, it provides
explicit uncertainty quantification around the synthetic control via wild-bootstrap
inference, transparently reporting pre-treatment RMSPE and acknowledging the limitations
of a single donor receiving full weight. Third, the Japan-calibrated counterfactual
projection provides a concrete, empirically grounded policy benchmark for Korean policymakers
--- the first such projection explicitly calibrated from Japan's observed post-TSE reform
P/B trajectory and anchored to KOSPI's 2024 base level.

Several directions merit future research. Firm-level panel analysis combining chaebol-specific
governance indices with market-level data would allow estimation of the cross-sectional
governance--valuation relationship within Korea, complementing the country-level time-series
identification strategy employed here. As Korea's Value-Up Program matures and generates
multi-year post-announcement data (beyond the 2024--2025 early period available at the time
of writing), an event study of the Korean program analogous to this paper's Japan analysis
would provide direct causal evidence on Korean governance reform. Extension to other
discount markets --- China A-shares, India, Gulf Cooperation Council --- would test whether
the structural channels identified for Korea generalize to other civil-law, concentrated-ownership
equity markets.

\section{Policy Recommendations}
\label{sec:policy}
%% ============================================================

The empirical evidence supports a set of specific, sequenced policy recommendations for
Korean policymakers. The recommendations are organized around the three institutional levers
most directly responsible for the Korea Discount: the Financial Services Commission (FSC),
the Korea Exchange (KRX), and the Stewardship Code framework. All three recommendations
draw on Japan's reform experience as an empirical benchmark.

\subsection{Recommendations}

\textbf{1. FSC Mandatory Capital Efficiency Disclosure.} The Financial Services Commission
should require all KOSPI-listed companies trading below $1.0\times$ book value to disclose
multi-year P/B improvement plans on a comply-or-explain basis, directly mirroring the 2023
TSE P/B reform that provides the empirical benchmark for this paper's counterfactual
projection. The Korea Corporate Value-Up Program (2024) moves in this direction but remains
voluntary; the compliance rate with voluntary programs in governance reform contexts is
typically lower than with mandatory comply-or-explain frameworks \citep{kcmi2023}. A
mandatory FSC requirement, with KRX-enforced disclosure penalties for non-compliance, would
replicate the mechanism by which Japan's TSE reform generated immediate and sustained P/B
convergence.

The FSC disclosure requirement should include: (i) a baseline assessment of the company's
cost of equity capital and current book-value efficiency metrics; (ii) specific medium-term
targets for P/B, return on equity (ROE), and dividend payout or buyback yield; (iii) an
explanation of the specific operational and capital structure actions the company will take
to achieve the targets; and (iv) annual progress reports with board-level accountability.
This mirrors the ``capital cost and stock price awareness'' disclosure framework introduced
by the TSE in March 2023 and extended in January 2024.

\textbf{2. KRX Listing Standards Reform.} The Korea Exchange should amend its listing
standards and corporate governance guidelines to strengthen board independence requirements
and related-party transaction oversight. Specifically: (i) large-cap KOSPI issuers (by
market capitalization) should be required to have a minimum 50\% independent board
composition, compared to the current standard of one-third; (ii) the KRX should introduce
listing rules requiring enhanced disclosure of material related-party transactions,
consistent with the thresholds that apply to NYSE-listed foreign private issuers; and
(iii) the audit committee requirements for chaebol-affiliated companies should be strengthened
to require independent audit committee majorities on transactions with controlling
shareholders. These measures reduce the principal--principal agency costs documented in
Section~\ref{sec:mechanisms} and increase the credibility of minority shareholder protection
that \citet{porta1998, porta2002} identify as the primary determinant of cross-country
valuation differences.

\textbf{3. Korean Stewardship Code Strengthening.} The Korean Stewardship Code (December
2016, revised 2018) should be amended to strengthen its active engagement and transparency
requirements. Specifically: (i) institutional investors subject to the Code --- including
the National Pension Service (NPS), domestic asset managers, and foreign managers with
Korean equity mandates --- should be required to publish annual voting records for every
shareholder meeting, including the rationale for votes against management recommendations;
(ii) asset managers should be required to engage publicly with investee companies that have
persistent P/B discounts below sector peers for three or more consecutive years; and (iii)
the NPS, as Korea's largest institutional investor, should establish a dedicated governance
engagement division with clear mandates to challenge controlling-shareholder decisions at
chaebol-affiliated companies that fail to improve P/B performance. Japan's experience shows
that Stewardship Code activation --- particularly the cross-shareholding unwinding documented
by \citet{miyajima2023} --- was a key transmission mechanism by which governance reform
translated into P/B improvement.

\subsection{Counterfactual Projection}

Based on Japan's observed P/B lift following the March 2023 TSE P/B reform --- as estimated
by the synthetic control gap series --- Figure~\ref{fig:figure4} presents an illustrative
projection of KOSPI P/B under the scenario that Korea implements an analogous mandatory
P/B governance reform. The projection starts from KOSPI's 2024 base level of approximately
$0.93\times$ book value and applies the average monthly TOPIX P/B change observed in the
12--18 months following Japan's March 2023 reform, projecting a hypothetical Korean reform
trajectory for approximately five years forward. The shaded uncertainty band reflects
$\pm$RMSPE ($= \pm 0.2893$) from the synthetic control estimation, which bounds the
uncertainty in the Japan post-reform gap estimate.

\textbf{This projection is illustrative.} It assumes that Korea's reform would produce a
P/B trajectory identical to Japan's post-2023 reform path, which may not hold given the
structural differences documented in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}: Korea's more extreme
chaebol concentration, FSC enforcement culture, and the persistent geopolitical risk premium
from North Korea (which governance reform alone cannot eliminate). The projection should be
interpreted as an order-of-magnitude policy benchmark --- demonstrating that a Japanese
reform outcome applied to Korea's starting P/B level would represent a meaningful valuation
improvement --- rather than a forecast of the actual outcome of any specific Korean policy
initiative.
